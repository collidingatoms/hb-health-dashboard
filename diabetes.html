Track 1 — Diabetic Retinopathy

Good news from the 31 Mar 2026 OCT macula scan: no diabetic macular edema visible in either eye, foveal contour preserved, RPE deformation maps clean, no drusen or subretinal fluid. Central macular thickness is well within the normal 250–300 μm band (R 282 μm, L 264 μm). For a patient with 20 years of diabetes and HbA1c that has lived above 10% for the last three years, this is a better result than expected.

What this OCT does and doesn't cover: it is excellent at detecting macular edema and neovascular changes behind the retina, but a formal dilated fundus exam is still the gold standard for staging early non-proliferative diabetic retinopathy (micro-aneurysms, dot-blot hemorrhages, cotton-wool spots). That wasn't done on this visit. Schedule one on the next eye appointment — ideally combined with the 1-month HVF recheck — so the DR side of the picture is fully locked down.

Forward risk: the DCCT/UKPDS data says every 1% HbA1c drop cuts retinopathy risk by ~35%. The 2.1-point drop from Aug 2025 → Apr 2026 has already bought meaningful protection. Keep pushing HbA1c below 9% and the next OCT a year from now should still be clean.

Track 2 — Glaucoma-suspect (new finding, under evaluation)

The unexpected finding of this visit. The OCT optic nerve head scan shows bilaterally enlarged cupping: cup-to-disc area ratios of 0.48 (R) and 0.55 (L), both above the 0.10–0.40 normal range. Vertical C/D ratios are 0.74 and 0.76 — clinically elevated. The Disc Damage Likelihood Scale (DDLS) at 4 and 5 suggests moderate likelihood of glaucomatous structural change. In parallel, the Humphrey 24-2 visual field on the right eye returned GHT Outside Normal Limits with a cluster of defects in the upper field; the left eye was clean.

GCL+IPL thickness: mean 86 μm (R) and 82 μm (L) — still in range on average, but the minimum thickness on the left eye is only 23 μm, indicating a focal region of ganglion cell loss that's worth rechecking at the 1-month visit.

Why the screening isn't over yet: a single VF "outside normal limits" result can be driven by fixation losses, learning effect, or artifact. Dr Pandey is correctly asking for a repeat HVF after a month before committing to a diagnosis. The prescribed regimen (Tab Forslin oral + Eye Drops TIZTA three times daily + Gel Cladra at bedtime) will either protect against progression during the observation window or give supporting clinical evidence when the next VF is read. Until the second HVF is in, this stays on the dashboard as suspect — under evaluation, not confirmed glaucoma.

What to do: complete the full 30-day regimen exactly as prescribed (Tab Forslin once daily after food, TIZTA drops three times daily, Cladra gel once a night), book the repeat bilateral HVF for end-April / early-May, and ask Dr Pandey to add a dilated fundus exam at that visit so the DR screening is formally on the record. If the second HVF is also abnormal, the next step is typically IOP series, gonioscopy, and possibly a 10-2 visual field.

Neuropathy Risk

Probable — untested
Assessment — diabetic neuropathy

With 20 years of diabetes and HbA1c still at 11.8%, diabetic peripheral neuropathy is likely present to some degree. Studies show 50% of diabetics develop neuropathy within 25 years, and risk rises with poor glycemic control.

No nerve conduction study (NCS) or monofilament testing data is on file. The recent hypothyroidism compounds the risk — hypothyroid neuropathy overlaps with diabetic neuropathy, causing additional numbness / tingling. As TSH normalises, some nerve symptoms should ease.

Symptoms to watch for: tingling, burning, or numbness in feet / hands (especially at night); loss of temperature / pain sensation; foot ulcers that don't heal; balance problems; digestive issues (gastroparesis).

Action: request a monofilament test and ankle reflex assessment at the next endocrinology visit. If symptoms are present, NCV testing confirms. Daily foot inspection is essential. B12 at 721 pg/mL (Apr 2026) is good for nerve health — keep that level up especially if Metformin is on board (it depletes B12).
