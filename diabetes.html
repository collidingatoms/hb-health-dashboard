Peripheral pigmentation is an early sign of diabetic retinopathy. With 20 years of diabetes and HbA1c consistently above 10% for the last 3 years, retinopathy is virtually guaranteed to some degree. The DCCT trial showed that every 1% reduction in HbA1c reduces retinopathy risk by 35%.

The Cup-to-Disc ratio of 0.6–0.7 is borderline — above 0.7 raises concern for glaucoma (which is more common in diabetics). 🧬 Thyroid — The Cascade Effect

Severe

TSH Trajectory — Exponential Rise

🔬 Clinical Deep Dive — Hypothyroidism

TSH at 62.7 with Free T4 at 0.69 and Free T3 at 2.26 is overt severe hypothyroidism. This isn't borderline — the thyroid has essentially stopped producing adequate hormones. The likely cause is Hashimoto's thyroiditis (autoimmune), which is more common in diabetics.

How this worsens everything:

  • Diabetes: Hypothyroidism increases insulin resistance by 30–50%, making glucose control nearly impossible at this TSH level
  • Lipids: Low thyroid directly raises LDL and triglycerides by slowing hepatic clearance — your lipids cannot normalize until thyroid is treated
  • Energy/Weight: Severe hypothyroidism causes fatigue, weight gain, constipation, brain fog — all of which make lifestyle changes harder
  • Kidneys: Hypothyroidism reduces renal blood flow, worsening the kidney stress from diabetes
  • Heart: Raises cholesterol, increases blood pressure, and can cause pericardial effusion in severe cases
  • Mental Health: Severe hypothyroidism mimics and worsens depression and anxiety

Treatment: Levothyroxine is the standard treatment. Starting dose is typically 1.6 µg/kg/day. For severe hypothyroidism, start at a lower dose (25–50 µg/day) and titrate up every 4–6 weeks to avoid cardiac stress. Recheck TSH and Free T4 at 6 weeks. Target TSH: 0.5–2.5 uIU/mL. Anti-TPO antibodies should be tested to confirm Hashimoto's.

🧬 Neuropathy Risk

Probable — Untested

🔬 Assessment — Diabetic Neuropathy

With 20 years of diabetes and sustained HbA1c >10%, diabetic peripheral neuropathy is very likely present. Studies show 50% of diabetics develop neuropathy within 25 years, and the risk increases dramatically with poor glycemic control.

No nerve conduction study (NCS) or monofilament testing data is available in your records. The hypothyroidism compounds the risk — hypothyroid neuropathy can overlap with diabetic neuropathy, causing additional numbness, tingling, and pain in extremities.

Symptoms to watch for: Tingling, burning, or numbness in feet/hands (especially at night); loss of sensation to temperature or pain; foot ulcers that don't heal; balance problems; digestive issues (gastroparesis).

Action: Request a monofilament test and ankle reflex assessment at your next endocrinologist visit. If symptoms are present, nerve conduction velocity (NCV) testing can confirm the diagnosis. Daily foot inspection is essential to prevent diabetic foot complications. Vitamin B12 at 378–688 pg/mL is adequate but could be optimized to >500 for nerve health, especially if taking Metformin (which depletes B12).
